Context: Actuarial Life Table (Sweden Data)

A life table tracks a hypothetical cohort of 100,000 individuals from birth, showing how many survive to specific ages based on current mortality rates. Hover over the chart to explore the experimental data.

Question 4

Data Interpretation
Using the Life Table chart above, Calculate the experimental probability that a person who has already survived to age 40 will survive to age 80. Express your answer as a percentage to one decimal place.

Justify how this experimental probability differs conceptually from flipping a coin, referring to the Statement of Inquiry.
Markscheme:
  • Identify survivors at Age 40: \( l_{40} = 98,000 \) (1 mark)
  • Identify survivors at Age 80: \( l_{80} = 65,000 \) (1 mark)
  • Calculate conditional probability: \( P(\text{Survive to 80} \mid \text{Survived to 40}) = \frac{65,000}{98,000} \approx 66.3\% \) (1 mark)
  • Justification: Flipping a coin is theoretical probability (based on pure logic/symmetry). The life table uses experimental probability (based on accumulated human experience and empirical data). Mortality is not a random uniform event but depends on complex biological/environmental factors. (2 marks)
Olympiad Pitfall: Many students calculate \( \frac{65,000}{100,000} = 65\% \). They fail to recognize the conditional nature of the prompt ("already survived to age 40"). The sample space has shrunk!

Question 5

Inclusion-Exclusion
In an MYP cohort of 150 students, data is collected on three subjects: Math (\(M\)), Physics (\(P\)), and Chemistry (\(C\)).

  • \( |M| = 80 \), \( |P| = 60 \), \( |C| = 50 \)
  • \( |M \cap P| = 30 \), \( |M \cap C| = 25 \), \( |P \cap C| = 20 \)
  • 10 students take all three subjects.

Calculate the probability that a randomly selected student takes at least two of these subjects, given that they take at least one.
Markscheme:
  • Find denominator (At least 1): Use PIE.
    \( |M \cup P \cup C| = 80 + 60 + 50 - 30 - 25 - 20 + 10 = 125 \). (2 marks)
  • Find numerator (At least 2):
    Regions representing exactly 2:
    Only M&P = 30 - 10 = 20
    Only M&C = 25 - 10 = 15
    Only P&C = 20 - 10 = 10
    Sum of exactly 2 = 45. Add all 3 (10) = 55. (2 marks)
  • Probability = \( \frac{55}{125} = \frac{11}{25} \). (1 mark)
Olympiad Pitfall: Double counting the center region! When adding the intersections \( (30+25+20 = 75) \), the center (10) is counted three times. To get the number of students taking at least 2, do not just sum the pairwise intersections.

Section 3: Criterion B Investigation

Investigating Patterns: Generalizing Venn Diagram Regions.

The Setup

A standard Venn diagram with 2 overlapping sets (circles) divides the universal rectangular plane into a maximum of 4 distinct regions. A diagram with 3 overlapping sets divides the plane into a maximum of 8 distinct regions.

Part A (Identify)

If you were to construct a Venn diagram using 4 overlapping shapes, what is the maximum number of distinct regions the universal set would be divided into?

16 distinct regions. (Note: It is impossible to draw 4 mutually overlapping circles to create 16 regions; ellipses or other shapes are required, a classic topological trap!)

Part B (Generalize)

Write a generalized formula for \( R_n \), the maximum number of distinct regions formed by \( n \) intersecting sets in a Venn diagram.

\( R_n = 2^n \)

Part C (Justify - AoPS Level)

Using logical principles of sets (In/Out states) or combinatorics, Justify why your generalized formula works.

Markscheme (Combinatorial Justification):

For any element \( x \) in the Universal set \( U \), and for any specific set \( S_i \) (where \( i \) goes from 1 to \( n \)), the element \( x \) has exactly two possible states:

  1. It is Inside the set (\( x \in S_i \))
  2. It is Outside the set (\( x \notin S_i \))

Since there are \( n \) distinct sets, and each set offers 2 independent choices for any point's location, the Fundamental Counting Principle states there are \( 2 \times 2 \times 2 \dots \times 2 \) (\( n \) times) possible combinations of states.

Therefore, there are \( 2^n \) mathematically distinct regions. (Level 7/8 Justification)
