📚 Economics Fundamentals

What is Economics?

The study of scarcity and its implications for the consumption and production of goods and services, as well as the transfer of wealth. Economics enables you to understand the world around you—people, business, markets, and governments and their interconnectedness.

Scarcity

The biggest economic problem: limited resources to satisfy unlimited needs and wants.

Goods & Services

Goods are tangible products; Services are intangible work done or advice provided.

Market

A place (physical or virtual) where people buy and sell goods and services.

🇸🇪 Swedish Example

The Swedish housing market in Stockholm demonstrates scarcity—with high demand and limited supply, prices rise significantly.

📉 Law of Demand

Definition

The law of demand explains the inverse relationship between the price of a good/service and the quantity demanded.

↑ Price → ↓ Quantity Demanded | ↓ Price → ↑ Quantity Demanded

Why Does Demand Slope Downward?

  • 💰 Income Effect: When prices fall, consumers feel wealthier and buy more.
  • 📊 Substitution Effect: When prices rise, consumers switch to cheaper alternatives.
  • 🎯 Diminishing Marginal Utility: Each additional unit is less valuable to consumers.

The demand curve slopes downward: as price increases, quantity demanded decreases

Factors Influencing Demand

Factor Effect on Demand Swedish Example
Income ↑ Income → ↑ Demand (normal goods) Tax cut → more iPhone 15 purchases
Preferences Trends & fashion changes demand Oatly popularity surge in Sweden
Population More people → more demand Immigration to Sweden
Interest Rates ↓ Rates → ↑ Demand (borrowing) Riksbank lowers rates → car loans
Price of Related Goods Substitutes & complements affect demand Spotify vs. Apple Music

🇸🇪 Real Example: Oatly Milk

If the price of Oatly milk in ICA increases from 20 SEK to 35 SEK, many Swedish consumers will switch to cheaper store-brand alternatives, causing a movement up the demand curve.

↔️ Shifts in Demand

Movement vs. Shift

Type Cause Effect
Movement Change in PRICE Along the curve
Shift Change in NON-PRICE factors Entire curve moves

Shift Right (Increase in Demand)

  • ↑ Income
  • ↑ Preferences (trends & fashion)
  • ↑ Population
  • ↓ Interest Rates
D₀ → D₁: Quantity Demanded Increases, Price stays the same

An increase in demand shifts the entire curve to the RIGHT

Shift Left (Decrease in Demand)

  • ↓ Income
  • ↓ Preferences
  • ↓ Population
  • ↑ Interest Rates
D₀ → D₂: Quantity Demanded Decreases, Price stays the same

A decrease in demand shifts the entire curve to the LEFT

🇸🇪 Swedish Example: Riksbank Interest Rates

If the Riksbank raises interest rates, it becomes more expensive to borrow money for a car loan. Consequently, the demand for Volvo cars shifts to the LEFT.

📈 Law of Supply

Definition

The law of supply explains the direct relationship between the price of a good/service and the quantity supplied.

↑ Price → ↑ Quantity Supplied | ↓ Price → ↓ Quantity Supplied

Why Does Supply Slope Upward?

Higher prices give producers more profit incentive to produce more. At higher prices, producers can cover higher production costs and still make a profit.

The supply curve slopes upward: as price increases, quantity supplied increases

Factors Influencing Supply

Factor Effect on Supply
Cost of Production (COP) ↑ COP → ↓ Supply (inverse relationship)
Technology ↑ Technology → ↑ Supply
Number of Firms ↑ Firms → ↑ Supply
Taxes & Subsidies ↑ Taxes → ↓ Supply | ↑ Subsidies → ↑ Supply
Expectations Expected ↓ demand → ↓ supply
Mother Nature Disasters → ↓ Supply

🇸🇪 Real Example: Swedish Timber

If the global price of timber increases, Swedish forestry companies like Sveaskog will increase logging operations to sell more wood while it is highly profitable.

🇸🇪 Swedish Example: Northvolt

Electricity prices for factories rise sharply. For Northvolt (battery producer), this increases the Cost of Production (COP). Their supply curve shifts to the LEFT.

⚖️ Market Equilibrium

Definition

A state of balance between supply and demand where the quantity demanded equals quantity supplied.

Qₑ: Qₛ = Qᴅ (Equilibrium Quantity)

Importance of Equilibrium

  • Consumers know what they will pay and how much will be available
  • Producers know how much will sell and at what price
  • Graphically: Where supply and demand curves intersect

Equilibrium (E): Where supply meets demand — Qd = Qs

Disequilibrium

Condition Definition Result
Shortage Price too low (Qᴅ > Qₛ) Price increases
Surplus Price too high (Qₛ > Qᴅ) Price decreases

🇸🇪 Real Example: Nord Pool Electricity

The daily price of electricity on the Nord Pool exchange. The price fluctuates until the amount of power generated matches the amount Swedish homes and factories need.

💸 Inflation & Purchasing Power

What is Inflation?

A general increase in prices of products due to economic crisis, causing a fall in the purchasing power of money.

Why Prices Gradually Increase

  • Natural increase in demand
  • Population growth
  • Currency loses value over time

📊 Swedish Example

100,000 SEK in 2020 = 110,000 SEK in 2024 (same goods). If Person A only has 100,000 SEK in 2024, they are poorer. If they have 110,000 SEK, they're the same. If more than 110,000 SEK, they're wealthier.

Is Inflation Good?

When Inflation is Positive When Inflation is Negative
Indicates economic growth Erodes purchasing power
Higher demand → higher prices Hurts fixed-income earners
More profit for businesses Creates uncertainty

⚠️ Extreme Example: Zimbabwe

Hyperinflation in Zimbabwe showed the devastating effects of uncontrolled money printing—prices doubled every few days.

🏦 Central Banks & Interest Rates

What is a Central Bank?

The "Bank of Banks"—run by the government to ensure prices don't fluctuate too much and to help manage the economy.

Low Interest Rates

  • ↑ Borrowing
  • ↑ Business growth
  • ↑ Consumer spending
  • Risk: Can cause inflation

High Interest Rates

  • ↓ Borrowing
  • ↓ Consumer spending
  • Used to combat inflation
  • Can lead to recession if too high

Recession

An economic depression causing GDP to fall as business success rates reduce and unemployment rates increase.

  • Causes: High inflation, War, Pandemic, High interest rates
  • Response: Central bank decreases interest rates

🇸🇪 Swedish Example: Riksbank

The Riksbank uses interest rates to control inflation. If inflation is too high, they raise rates (like raising rates from 2% to 4% affects Volvo car demand).

🏛️ Government Intervention

Price Ceilings

Government-imposed maximum price that producers can charge for a product (usually for essential goods).

  • Price set BELOW equilibrium
  • ↑ Demand + ↓ Supply = SHORTAGE

Price ceiling creates a shortage — quantity demanded exceeds quantity supplied

🇸🇪 Example: Stockholm Rent Control

Rent control keeps prices below equilibrium, resulting in long queues (shortages) for apartments in Stockholm.

Price Floors

Government-imposed minimum price to protect producers/farmers.

  • Price set ABOVE equilibrium
  • ↓ Demand + ↑ Supply = SURPLUS

Price floor creates a surplus — quantity supplied exceeds quantity demanded

🇸🇪 Example: Swedish Agricultural Subsidies

Minimum corn price leads to excess supply that government must buy.

🌍 Global Case Studies

🛢️ Middle East Conflict - Oil Market

Iran borders the Strait of Hormuz through which 20% of global oil supply flows. When the strait was blocked, supply ↓ rapidly causing sharp ↑ in price. As a secondary effect, countries worry about running out of oil, leading to panic buying → demand ↑.

🌽 Corn Industry Failure

When crop farming industries fail, supply decreases. This eventually results in increasing demand for US corn farming industry. Heavy demand causes price to increase.

⛏️ DRC Cobalt Crisis

Political instability in the Democratic Republic of Congo (produces 70% of global cobalt) reduces cobalt supply by 40%. Since cobalt is key for EV batteries, input costs increase → EV prices rise in Sweden (Volvo, Polestar).

📋 Key Formulas Summary

Demand

  • Law of Demand: ↑P → ↓Qᴅ (inverse)
  • Demand Shift Right: ↑Income, ↑Preferences, ↑Population, ↓Interest Rates
  • Demand Shift Left: ↓Income, ↓Preferences, ↓Population, ↑Interest Rates

Supply

  • Law of Supply: ↑P → ↑Qₛ (direct)
  • Supply Shift Right: ↓COP, ↑Technology, ↑Subsidies, ↓Taxes
  • Supply Shift Left: ↑COP, ↓Technology, ↑Taxes, ↓Subsidies

Equilibrium

  • Equilibrium: Qᴅ = Qₛ
  • Shortage: Qᴅ > Qₛ (price too low)
  • Surplus: Qₛ > Qᴅ (price too high)
